# Chapter 2: Payment Collection -- Who Owes You Money?

---

## What This Page Tells You

Here is a simple truth that catches a lot of people off guard: **selling stuff is not the same as getting paid**.

Hoi-Yong Finance sells about RM 88 million worth of fruits every year. That is a big number. But here is the uncomfortable question this page answers: *how much of that money has actually landed in the bank account?*

Most of Hoi-Yong's customers do not pay immediately. They are businesses -- supermarkets, restaurants, chain stores -- and they buy on **credit**. That means Hoi-Yong delivers a truckload of mangoes, sends an invoice, and then waits 30 to 60 days (sometimes longer) for the customer to pay. During that wait, the money exists only on paper. The fruits are gone, but the cash has not arrived.

This page tracks three things:

1. **Who owes money** -- and how much
2. **How long they have owed it** -- because older debts are harder to collect
3. **How risky each customer is** -- so you know who might never pay at all

Think of this as the dashboard's "collections department." If the Sales page (Chapter 1) is about how much business you are doing, the Payment Collection page is about how much of that business is actually turning into cash you can spend.

> **Why should you care?**
>
> A company can be selling millions of ringgit worth of products and still go bankrupt. How? If customers are not paying, you cannot pay your own suppliers, your staff, your rent. Cash flow -- the actual movement of money in and out -- is what keeps a business alive. Revenue is vanity; cash flow is sanity.

## Key Concept: Accounts Receivable (AR)

Before we dive into the charts, you need to understand one term: **Accounts Receivable**, usually shortened to **AR**.

AR is the total amount of money that customers owe you right now. You delivered the goods, you sent the invoice, and now you are waiting for the cheque (or bank transfer). That waiting money is your Accounts Receivable.

Here is an everyday analogy. Imagine you lend RM 50 to your friend Ali on Monday. He says he will pay you back on Friday. Between Monday and Friday, Ali "owes" you RM 50 -- that is your personal Accounts Receivable. If Friday comes and goes and Ali still has not paid, that RM 50 is now **overdue**. If three months pass and Ali keeps dodging your messages, that RM 50 is probably never coming back.

Now scale that up. Instead of one friend, Hoi-Yong has hundreds of customers. Instead of RM 50, the total amount owed is **RM 11.3 million**. And instead of one Friday deadline, there are thousands of invoices with different due dates.

Two key terms:

- **Outstanding** = money that customers have not paid yet (some of it might not be due yet, and that is fine)
- **Overdue** = money that is PAST the due date and still not paid (this is the problem)

### 1. Total Outstanding -- RM 11,369,300 (Orange Card)

Think of it as the total IOU pile sitting on your desk. Some of these IOUs are not due yet (the customer still has time to pay), and some are overdue. But together, they add up to RM 11.3 million that is out there, waiting to come back.

This number is a **snapshot** -- it shows the current state regardless of what date range you pick at the top of the page.

### 2. Overdue Amount -- RM 11,369,300, 100% (Red Card)

**What it means:** Of that RM 11.3 million outstanding, how much is past due?

In Hoi-Yong's case, the answer is... all of it. 100%. Every single ringgit that customers owe is overdue. Not a single outstanding invoice is within its payment deadline.

> **Red flags to watch for:**
>
> If the overdue percentage is above 50%, something is seriously wrong with collection. If it is at 100% like we see here, it means either payment terms are too generous, collection efforts are too relaxed, or customers are simply ignoring their invoices. This is a five-alarm fire for cash flow.

### 3. Days Sales Outstanding (DSO) -- 133 days (Green/Yellow/Red Card)

**What it means:** On average, it takes 133 days to collect payment after sending an invoice. That is about four and a half months.

DSO is one of the most important numbers in business finance, so let us break it down carefully.

**The simple version:** DSO answers the question "after I send an invoice, how many days until I get paid?"

**How to think about it:** If your payment terms say "Net 30" (meaning customers should pay within 30 days), then a good DSO would be somewhere around 30 to 45 days. A DSO of 133 days means customers are paying, on average, about 100 days late. That is over three months of extra waiting.

**An analogy:** Imagine your boss says you will be paid on the 1st of every month. But the money does not actually show up until May 13th for your January salary. You would be pretty upset, right? That is what a DSO of 133 feels like for a business.

**The colour coding:**
- Green = 30 days or less (great, customers pay on time)
- Yellow = 31 to 60 days (okay-ish, a bit slow)
- Red = over 60 days (trouble -- and 133 is deep in the red zone)

**For a fruit distributor specifically,** DSO should be low. Fruits are perishable -- they get sold quickly by the customer. There is no reason a supermarket should take 133 days to pay for bananas it sold months ago. A healthy DSO for this industry is 30 to 45 days.

### 4. Collection Rate -- 85.7% (Green/Yellow/Red Card)

**What it means:** For every RM 100 that Hoi-Yong invoiced during the selected period, RM 85.70 was actually collected.

**The maths:** Collection Rate = Total Collected / Total Invoiced x 100

An 85.7% collection rate sounds decent at first glance. But think about what the missing 14.3% means. If you invoiced RM 88 million over a year, that 14.3% gap is about RM 12.6 million that has not come in. That is not loose change.

**The colour coding:**
- Green = 80% or above (you are collecting most of what you bill)
- Yellow = 50% to 79% (a lot of money is stuck)
- Red = below 50% (half your sales are not turning into cash)

### 5. Credit Limit Breaches -- 21 (Red Card)

**What it means:** 21 customers currently owe MORE than their approved credit limit.

**What is a credit limit?** It is the maximum amount you are willing to let a customer owe you at any time. If you set a customer's credit limit at RM 100,000, that means you are comfortable with them owing up to RM 100K. If they owe RM 120K, they have "breached" their limit.

**Why it matters:** A credit limit is your safety net. When a customer goes over it, you are exposed to more risk than you agreed to take on. These 21 customers should probably not receive any more goods on credit until they pay down their balance.

**The colour coding:**
- Green = 0 breaches (everyone is within limits)
- Red = any number above 0

### 6. Avg Monthly Collection -- RM 6,381,543 (Blue Card)

**What it means:** Over the selected period, Hoi-Yong collected about RM 6.4 million per month on average.

This gives you a feel for the "speed" of cash coming in. You can compare it to monthly expenses -- if the business spends RM 7 million per month but only collects RM 6.4 million, there is a monthly shortfall of RM 600K. That shortfall has to come from somewhere (savings, loans, or delayed payments to suppliers).

## DSO Trend Chart

### How to Read This Chart

This is a line chart that shows DSO for each month over your selected date range. There is also a dashed horizontal line showing the average DSO across all displayed months.

- **X-axis** (horizontal): months, like 2025-01, 2025-02, etc.
- **Y-axis** (vertical): DSO in days
- **Solid line**: the DSO value for each month
- **Dashed line**: the average DSO (labelled "Avg" followed by the number)

**If the line is going UP over time**, customers are paying slower. Collection is getting worse. This is like watching your friend Ali push back the payback date from Friday to next week to next month.

**If the line is going DOWN**, collection is improving. Customers are paying faster.

**If the line is flat**, nothing is changing -- which could be fine if DSO is low, or alarming if DSO is stuck at 133 days.

> **Red flags to watch for:**
>
> - A sustained upward trend over 3+ months -- something systemic is wrong
> - Sudden spikes -- a large customer might have stopped paying
> - DSO consistently above 60 days for a produce business -- this is too slow

> **How to read this chart:**
>
> Look at the overall direction first (up, down, or flat). Then check where the line sits relative to the dashed average. Months above the average are worse-than-usual collection months; months below are better.

## Invoiced vs Collected Chart

### How to Read This Chart

This is a combination chart: blue bars show how much was **collected** each month, and a red line shows how much was **invoiced** each month. There is also a dashed reference line at the average monthly collection.

- **Blue bars** = cash that actually came in (payments received)
- **Red line** = new invoices sent out (new money owed to you)

**The ideal picture:** The blue bars should be at or above the red line. That means you are collecting money at least as fast as you are creating new invoices. You are keeping up.

**The warning sign:** If the red line is consistently above the blue bars, it means you are invoicing faster than you are collecting. Cash is "leaking" -- the gap between what you are owed and what you are receiving is growing every month. Over time, this leads to a cash crunch.

**An analogy:** Imagine a bathtub. The red line is the tap (water flowing in -- new invoices). The blue bars are the drain (water flowing out -- payments collected). If the tap runs faster than the drain, the tub overflows. In business terms, the "overflow" is a dangerous pile-up of unpaid invoices.

> **When someone asks you...**
>
> "Are we collecting faster or slower than we are selling?" -- This is the chart to show them. Point to whether the blue bars are keeping up with the red line.

## Aging Analysis

### The Concept

The Aging Analysis chart breaks down all outstanding invoices by how overdue they are. It uses "buckets" -- time ranges that tell you how old each unpaid invoice is.

Think of it like checking the fridge. Food that is one day old? Fine. One week old? Probably still okay. One month old? Getting risky. Four months old? It is gone bad. Throw it out.

Unpaid invoices work the same way. The older they get, the less likely you are to collect them.

### The Buckets

| Bucket | What It Means | Colour | Worry Level |
|--------|--------------|--------|-------------|
| **Not Yet Due** | The invoice is not past its due date yet. The customer still has time. | Green | No worry |
| **1-30 Days** | Slightly overdue. Could just be a slow week. | Yellow | Mild nudge needed |
| **31-60 Days** | A month late. Time to follow up seriously. | Orange | Concerned |
| **61-90 Days** | Two to three months late. This is a real problem. | Light Red | Worried |
| **91-120 Days** | Three to four months late. Getting close to write-off territory. | Red | Very worried |
| **120+ Days** | Over four months late. This money might never come back. | Dark Red | Alarm bells |

### How to Read This Chart

The chart shows horizontal bars, one for each bucket. The length of each bar represents the total RM amount in that bucket.

**What you want to see:** Most of the money in the "Not Yet Due" bucket (green), with small amounts in the overdue buckets.

**What Hoi-Yong actually shows:** Most of the RM 11.3 million is concentrated in the 120+ days bucket. This means the majority of outstanding money is very old debt that has been sitting unpaid for over four months. In a fruit distribution business where products are sold and consumed within days, having invoices unpaid for 120+ days is a serious problem.

### Toggle Views

You can switch between three views using buttons at the top of the chart:

- **All** -- shows the simple total per bucket
- **By Agent** -- breaks each bar into segments by sales agent, so you can see which agent's customers owe the most in each bucket
- **By Type** -- breaks each bar by customer type (Chain Store, Restaurant, etc.)

The "By Agent" view is particularly useful for accountability. If one agent's customers dominate the 120+ bucket, that agent might need to be more careful about who they sell to on credit.

> **Red flags to watch for:**
>
> - The 120+ bucket being the largest -- this is exactly the situation at Hoi-Yong, and it is concerning
> - The "Not Yet Due" bucket being tiny relative to the overdue buckets
> - Any single agent or customer type dominating the older buckets

## The Credit Scoring Algorithm

This is one of the most powerful features on this page. Every customer gets a **credit score** from 0 to 100, like a school exam grade. The score tells you, at a glance, how risky it is to keep extending credit to this customer.

### How It Works: Five Factors

Think of the credit score like a school report card with five subjects. Each subject has a different weight (some count more toward your final grade than others), and the weighted average gives you the overall score.

#### Factor 1: Credit Utilization -- 35% of the grade

**What it measures:** How much of their credit limit are they using?

This is the biggest factor because it directly measures exposure. A customer using 20% of their limit is much safer than one using 150%.

| Usage Level | Component Score | Analogy |
|-------------|----------------|---------|
| 0-50% of limit | 90-100 | Using a small fraction of the limit. Very safe. |
| 51-80% | 60-89 | Moderate usage. Still okay. |
| 81-100% | 30-59 | Near the edge. Getting risky. |
| Over 100% | 0-29 | Already past the limit. Danger zone. |

If no credit limit is set, the customer gets a 0 for this factor (treated as unknown, which is risky).

#### Factor 2: Overdue Days -- 25% of the grade

**What it measures:** How old is their oldest unpaid invoice?

The longer an invoice goes unpaid, the less likely it is to ever be collected. This factor punishes customers with very old debts.

| Oldest Unpaid Invoice | Component Score |
|-----------------------|----------------|
| Nothing overdue | 100 (perfect!) |
| 1-30 days late | 80 |
| 31-60 days late | 60 |
| 61-90 days late | 40 |
| 91-120 days late | 20 |
| Over 120 days late | 0 (failing) |

#### Factor 3: Payment Consistency -- 15% of the grade

**What it measures:** Do they pay every month, or do they skip months?

This looks at the last 12 months and checks: out of the months they were invoiced, how many months did they also make a payment?

| Payment Ratio | Component Score | Translation |
|--------------|----------------|-------------|
| 90%+ of months | 100 | They pay almost every month. Reliable. |
| 70-89% | 75 | They miss a month here and there. |
| 50-69% | 50 | They skip quite a few months. Inconsistent. |
| Below 50% | 25 | They rarely pay. Very unreliable. |

**Analogy:** It is like checking whether a friend who borrows money actually sends you something back regularly, or only pays when you chase them down.

#### Factor 4: Payment Timeliness -- 15% of the grade

**What it measures:** When they DO pay, are they on time or late?

This is different from consistency. A customer might pay every month (consistent) but always two weeks late (not timely).

| Average Days Late | Component Score |
|-------------------|----------------|
| On time or early | 100 |
| 1-7 days late | 80 |
| 8-14 days late | 60 |
| 15-30 days late | 40 |
| 31-60 days late | 20 |
| Over 60 days late | 0 |

#### Factor 5: Limit Breach -- 10% of the grade

**What it measures:** Are they currently over their credit or overdue limit?

This is a simple yes/no check. Either they have breached a limit or they have not.

| Status | Component Score |
|--------|----------------|
| Not breached | 100 |
| Breached | 0 |

### Putting It All Together

The final score is calculated by weighting each factor:

```
Score = (Utilization Score x 35%) + (Overdue Days Score x 25%)
      + (Consistency Score x 15%) + (Timeliness Score x 15%)
      + (Breach Score x 10%)
```

### What the Score Means: Risk Tiers

Once the score is calculated, it maps to a risk level -- like translating a numeric grade into a letter grade:

| Score Range | Risk Tier | What It Means | Colour |
|-------------|-----------|---------------|--------|
| 85-100 | **Low Risk** | The A student. Reliable payer, low exposure, pays on time. Keep selling to them with confidence. | Green |
| 65-84 | **Moderate Risk** | The B student. Mostly okay, but worth keeping an eye on. Maybe a few late payments or creeping close to their limit. | Yellow |
| 0-64 | **High Risk** | The C/D/F student. Significant problems -- very late, over limit, inconsistent. Consider stopping credit sales until they improve. | Red |

### Real Example: MY HERO HYPERMARKET (Score: 14)

Let us see why this customer scored so poorly:

- **Credit Utilization**: 163% of limit. Way over. Score for this factor: very low (probably around 0-10).
- **Overdue Days**: 411 days. That is over a year. Score: 0.
- **Consistency**: Unknown from this view, but likely low given the other numbers.
- **Timeliness**: With invoices 411 days old, they are clearly not paying on time. Score: 0.
- **Breach**: They are RM 800K over their credit limit. Score: 0.

Weighted together, you get 14 out of 100. This customer is a very high risk.

> **Why should you care?**
>
> The credit score lets you make data-driven decisions about who to sell to on credit. Without it, you are relying on gut feeling. With it, you have a number you can point to and say: "This customer scores 14. We should not ship another box of oranges to them until they pay down their balance."

**30 to 45 days** is healthy for the produce distribution industry. Products are perishable and turn over quickly, so there is no reason for slow payment. Hoi-Yong's DSO of 133 days is roughly 3 to 4 times what it should be.
